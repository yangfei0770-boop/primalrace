道歉是结构性暴力的廉价润滑剂Apologies as Cheap Lubricants for Structural Violence

国际 结构层 · 文化层 · 元暴力 The Guardian ↗ 2026-07-23 § 链接
所谓的“错过机会”本质上是对极端主义的结构性纵容。
The so-called 'missed opportunity' is essentially a structural indulgence of extremism.

警察局长 Sir Stephen Watson 所谓的 “missed opportunity” 是一个典型的武器化叙事。通过将严重的职能失效定义为“错过”，他把一次结构性的安全溃败包装成了一个偶然的运气问题。在暴力三角中，这属于典型的 structural violence：当执法机构掌握了能预警恐怖袭击的电子设备却选择不审查，这种“不作为”本身就是一种暴力，它直接导致了 Actual 状态与 Potential 安全状态之间巨大的差额。

这里的共谋机制非常清晰。警方在逮捕 Jihad al-Shamie 两次之后依然没有审查其设备，说明在执法者的认知入口中，这类极端主义的信号要么被视为低优先级，要么在某种潜意识的文化共谋中被视作“可接受的背景噪音”。直到袭击发生、死伤出现，这种不作为才被定义为“遗憾”。

法律程序结束后的道歉，不过是权力机构在面对公众压力时的一种表演性让步。它试图通过承认一个技术性的失误（没看手机），来掩盖更深层的元暴力——即权力机器在面对特定意识形态暴力时，其反应机制的迟钝与傲慢。这种道歉不需要承担结构性的责任，只需要用一个 “deeply sorry” 就能维持系统运行的合法性。

真正的 question 不是他们是否错过了机会，而是这套让极端主义者在被捕两次后依然能自由策划袭击的系统，究竟在为谁服务，又在共谋什么样的沉默。

Sir Stephen Watson’s framing of a “missed opportunity” is a textbook example of weaponized expression. By defining a severe functional failure as a “missed chance,” he packages a structural security collapse as a mere accident of luck. In the Violence Triangle, this is pure structural violence: when law enforcement seizes devices capable of alerting them to a terrorist attack but chooses not to examine them, this “inaction” is itself a form of violence, creating a lethal gap between the actual state and the potential safety.

The mechanism of complicity here is transparent. The fact that police arrested Jihad al-Shamie twice yet still failed to review his devices suggests that, within the cognitive entry points of law enforcement, these signals of extremism were either treated as low-priority or dismissed as “acceptable background noise” through a subconscious cultural complicity. Only after the attack and the subsequent casualties was this negligence rebranded as a “regret.”

An apology issued after legal proceedings are concluded is nothing more than a performative concession by a power structure facing public pressure. It attempts to substitute a technical error (not checking a phone) for a deeper meta-violence—the inherent sluggishness and arrogance of the state machine when dealing with specific ideological violence. Such apologies require no structural accountability; a simple “deeply sorry” suffices to maintain the legitimacy of the system.

The real question is not whether they missed an opportunity, but why a system that allows extremists to plan attacks after two arrests still functions. Who is this system serving, and what silence is it complicit in?
