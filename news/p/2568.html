用“数亿次飞行”掩盖一个人的头颅Using 'Millions of Flights' to Erase a Single Head

国际 直接层 · 结构层 · 文化层 · 元暴力 The Guardian ↗ 2026-07-20 § 链接
用统计学的概率去对冲个体的生存危机，是典型的结构暴力 PR 话术。
Using statistical probability to hedge individual survival crises is a classic structural violence PR tactic.

一个 61 岁的男人头先被吸出机窗，这是最直接的 direct violence。但 Ryanair CFO 的回应是一次教科书级的 cultural violence：他用“全球数亿次飞行”这个宏大叙事，试图把一个近乎死亡的个体悲剧，稀释成一个微不足道的统计学概率。在企业的逻辑里，只要概率足够低，个体的惊恐就成了“无需担心”的噪音。

这种叙事方式本质上是在夺取解释权。CFO 迅速将话题从“窗户为什么会碎”转移到“机组人员表现卓越”以及“波音 737 是最安全的飞机”。这是典型的 weaponized expression，通过重新定义“安全”——将安全定义为“符合监管标准”而非“不让乘客掉出去”——来掩盖结构性的维护漏洞。至于补偿？他说“现在讨论还太早”，这不过是在博弈中通过拖延来降低赔付成本的最优解表达。

最荒诞的共谋在于，这份声明在谈论乘客差点丧命的同时，竟然顺便抱怨了欧盟的数字入境系统（EES）导致排队太长。在 CFO 的认知地图里，乘客被吸出窗外的恐惧，竟然与在机场排队的烦躁被放在同一个“挑战”维度里讨论。这种对生命权的极度轻视，正是元暴力的体现：在资本的中心叙事中，人的主体性完全消失了，乘客只是一个被运送的数值，而安全只是一个为了维持 bookings strong 而需要维护的 PR 标签。

A 61-year-old man was nearly sucked out of a plane window headfirst—this is direct violence in its rawest form. However, Ryanair's CFO responded with a textbook display of cultural violence: using the grand narrative of "hundreds of millions of flights globally" to dilute an individual near-death tragedy into a negligible statistical probability. In corporate logic, as long as the probability is low enough, an individual's terror is dismissed as noise that "travellers did not need to worry about."

This approach is essentially an attempt to seize the power of interpretation. The CFO swiftly shifted the focus from "why the window shattered" to how the "crew did a phenomenal job" and the claim that the Boeing 737 is "the safest aircraft ever built." This is weaponized expression, redefining "safety" not as "not letting passengers fall out," but as "compliance with regulations," thereby masking structural maintenance failures. As for compensation? Claiming it is "way too early" to discuss is simply an optimal expression of delaying tactics to minimize payout costs in a power game.

The most absurd complicity lies in the fact that this statement, while addressing a passenger's near-death experience, simultaneously complains about the EU's digital entry-exit system (EES) causing long queues. In the CFO's cognitive map, the terror of being sucked out of a plane and the annoyance of queuing at an airport are treated as similar "challenges." This extreme disregard for the right to life is a manifestation of meta-violence: in the masculine-centric corporate narrative, human subjectivity vanishes. The passenger is merely a numerical unit to be transported, and "safety" is just a PR label maintained to keep bookings strong.
