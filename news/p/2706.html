被外包的肠胃风险与资本的PR闭环Outsourced Gut Risks and the Capitalist PR Loop

国际 结构层 · 文化层 The New York Times ↗ 2026-07-22 § 链接
结构性暴力就是让底层承担风险，让资本通过定义“事实”来免责。
Structural violence is making the bottom bear the risk while capital defines "facts" to evade liability.

这则新闻的切入点是某种荒诞的幽默感，但底色是典型的 structural violence。一个加州公司 Taylor Farms 在墨西哥瓜纳胡亚托州建立生产设施，本质上是将低端生产环节和潜在的生物风险外包给一个监管成本更低、劳动力更廉价的地区。当 Cyclospora 寄生虫让美国消费者的肠胃变成“定时炸弹”时，真正处于风险核心的不是那些吃沙拉的中产，而是像 Carmen Ramírez 这样依赖这家公司生存的当地居民，尤其是单亲母亲。对于她们来说，这次疫情不是一个 meme，而是生存权的博弈。

最精彩的部分在于结尾：Taylor Farms 试图通过一个“假阳性”测试结果来制造叙事，声称 FDA 已经道歉。这是一种典型的 weaponized expression。公司试图通过操纵认知入口，将一个系统性的公共卫生危机，简化为一个技术性的检测误差。它在争夺“什么是事实”的定义权——如果能把结论定义为“误报”，那么它就无需为结构性的卫生漏洞负责，也无需面对潜在的赔偿。

而 FDA 的否认则揭示了这场博弈的残酷：即便数据是 overwhelming 的，但在资本的 PR 机器面前，真相的传播速度永远慢于“道歉”这种叙事伪装。在这种共谋结构中，墨西哥小镇的工人成了最廉价的耗材：他们既要忍受生产环境的低劣，又要承担公司名誉受损带来的失业风险。在这种逻辑里，资本不仅垄断了利润，还垄断了对“真相”的定价权。

The news enters with a sense of absurd humor, but the undertone is classic structural violence. Taylor Farms, a California-based giant, established facilities in Guanajuato, Mexico, essentially outsourcing low-end production and biological risks to a region with lower regulatory costs and cheaper labor. When Cyclospora turned American bowels into "time bombs," the people at the core of the risk weren't the salad-eating middle class, but local residents like Carmen Ramírez, especially single mothers, whose livelihoods depend on this company. For them, this outbreak isn't a meme; it's an existential war over survival.

The most revealing part is the end: Taylor Farms attempted to manufacture a narrative using a "false positive" test result, claiming the FDA had apologized. This is a textbook case of weaponized expression. The company is trying to hijack the cognitive entry point, reducing a systemic public health crisis to a technical glitch. They are fighting for the power to define "what is fact"—if the conclusion can be framed as a "misreport," they avoid accountability for structural sanitary failures.

The FDA's denial exposes the cruelty of this game: even with overwhelming data, the truth moves slower than the PR machinery of a corporation. In this framework of complicity, the workers in a Mexican town are the cheapest consumables: they endure substandard environments and then bear the risk of unemployment when the company's reputation is hit. In this logic, capital monopolizes not only the profit but the pricing power of "truth."
