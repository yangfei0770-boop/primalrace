被剥夺的潜在状态：阿富汗洪水的结构性屠杀The Structural Massacre of Afghanistan's Floods

国际 结构层 · 文化层 The New York Times ↗ 2026-07-21 § 链接
自然灾害是背景，结构性暴力才是杀人凶手。
Natural disasters are the backdrop; structural violence is the actual killer.

23人死亡，数十人失踪，在常规新闻叙事里，这被标记为“突发洪水”带来的不幸。但如果套用加尔通的暴力三角，这就是一次典型的 structural violence。当一个省份在2026年依然处于“绝大多数地区没有铺装路面”且“通讯受限”的状态时，这种 isolation 本身就是一种暴力。救援队需要10到12小时才能到达，这意味着在最关键的黄金救援时间内，由于基础设施的缺失，潜在的生存率（Potential）与实际的存活率（Actual）之间存在巨大的差额。这个差额，就是由数十年战争、地缘博弈和治理失效共同构成的结构性暴力。

更讽刺的是这种暴力的 weaponization 逻辑。曾经有叙事称“为了解放中东女性”而开战，结果是阿富汗在经历20年战争后，基础设施被摧毁，女性被驱逐出公共空间，现在面对洪水的生存能力比战争前更低。所谓的“人道主义干预”在结果上变成了对该地区生存底线的系统性削弱。现在的局面是：美国削减援助，邻国强制遣返，塔利班在森林里建立军事单位打击非法伐木。这种“危机叠加危机”的现状，本质上是全球权力结构在完成其地缘博弈后，将该地区的个体抛弃在一个极低生存阈值的环境中。

不要被“非法伐木”或“气候变化”这种局部叙事遮蔽。当一个人因为没有路、没有电话、没有医疗保障而在泥石流中死去时，杀掉他的不是雨水，而是那个决定谁该被连接、谁该被隔离的权力分配机制。在这种机制里，阿富汗人的生命被定价为极低，因此他们的死亡被轻描淡写地归类为“年度经常性事件”。

23 dead, dozens missing. In a conventional news narrative, this is labeled as the misfortune of a "flash flood." But applying Galtung's Violence Triangle, this is a textbook case of structural violence. When a province in 2026 remains "not connected by paved roads" and "internet services remain limited," this isolation itself is violence. The 10-to-12-hour delay for rescue teams means a lethal gap exists between the potential for survival and the actual outcome. This gap is the accumulated structural violence of decades of war, geopolitical gambling, and systemic governance failure.

There is a cruel irony in the weaponization of these narratives. There was once a narrative that the war in Afghanistan was fought to "liberate women." The actual result: infrastructure destroyed, women erased from public life, and a population with a lower survival baseline than before the intervention. The so-called "humanitarian intervention" effectively functioned as a systematic stripping of the region's survival capacity. Now, with U.S. aid cuts and forced returns from neighbors, the "crisis on top of crisis" is simply the endgame of a global power structure that discarded these individuals after their geopolitical utility expired.

Do not let narratives about "illegal logging" or "climate change" obscure the core. When a person dies in a mudslide because there are no roads, no phones, and no healthcare, they are not killed by rain, but by the power mechanism that decides who is connected and who is isolated. In this system, Afghan lives are priced so low that their deaths are dismissed as "annual occurrences."
