用换掉人口来定义“可负担性”的统计骗局The Statistical Scam of 'Affordability' through Population Purge

哲学 结构层 · 文化层 The New York Times ↗ 2026-07-23 § 链接
所谓的“可负担性”提升，本质上是高净值人群对低端人口的物理清洗。
Increased 'affordability' is actually the physical cleansing of low-income populations by high-net-worth elites.

这篇报道揭露了一个典型的统计学 scam：当曼哈顿和旧金山的住房变得“更可负担”时，房子并没有变便宜，而是那些买不起房的人被清洗掉了。NAR 的指标将“住房支出占收入比”定义为可负担性，这在逻辑上极其阴险——它不追踪人口的流动，只记录幸存者的快感。

这是一种典型的 structural violence。系统通过推高房价，将低收入者强行驱逐出核心区域，然后用高薪的科技和金融从业者填补空缺。当一个区域的 median income 翻了三倍，而房价只翻了两倍时，数据会告诉你这里变得“更宜居”了。但事实是，这里变成了一个纯粹的富人俱乐部，原住民的生存空间被彻底抹除。

这种叙事最恶心的地方在于，它试图用一个数学比例来掩盖存在性战争的残酷。对于被驱逐的人来说，Potential（在城市中心生活并获取资源）与 Actual（被挤到郊区或贫民窟）之间的差额，就是由这种所谓的“可负担性提升”所制造的暴力。这不是经济增长，而是通过人口置换实现的阶级纯化。

所谓的“可负担”，其实是给富人量身定制的定价权。当一个地方被定义为“更可负担”时，它实际上在宣布：这里已经不再欢迎穷人了。

This report exposes a classic statistical scam: when Manhattan and San Francisco become 'more affordable,' the houses didn't get cheaper; the people who couldn't afford them were simply purged. The NAR metric defines affordability as the percentage of income spent on housing, a logically sinister move—it ignores population churn and only records the satisfaction of the survivors.

This is a textbook example of structural violence. The system drives up prices to forcibly evict low-income residents, replacing them with high-earning tech and finance professionals. When the median income triples while home values only double, the data claims the area is 'more livable.' In reality, it has become a pure plutocratic club where the existence of original residents is erased.

The most repulsive part of this narrative is the attempt to mask an existential war with a mathematical ratio. For those displaced, the gap between Potential (living and accessing resources in the city center) and Actual (being pushed to the outskirts) is the violence manufactured by this 'increase in affordability.' This isn't economic growth; it's class purification through population replacement.

This version of 'affordability' is merely a pricing power tailored for the wealthy. When a place is labeled 'more affordable,' it is effectively announcing: the poor are no longer welcome here.
