原油价格的跳涨与被抹除的血肉代价Oil Spikes and the Erasure of Flesh

国际 文化层 · 结构层 · 元暴力 The New York Times ↗ 2026-07-22 § 链接
战争被量化为油价曲线，而真实的暴力在叙事中被彻底客体化。
War is quantified as an oil curve, while actual violence is completely objectified in the narrative.

这是一篇标准的、充满元暴力的男性中心叙事报道。在《纽约时报》的笔触下，美国与伊朗的战争被简化成了一场关于“能源流向”和“油价轨迹”的金融博弈。Brent原油上涨2.5%，WTI突破86美元，这些精确的数字成为了报道的骨架，而战争中真实的死亡、被摧毁的电站以及被波及的平民身体，仅仅是支撑这些数字跳动的背景噪音。

这种叙事方式是典型的“表达武器化”。它通过将战争定义为“市场不确定性” (uncertainty)，成功地将直接暴力 (direct violence) 转化为一种结构性的经济波动。当报道在讨论胡塞武装是否会限制交通、中国购买多少原油时，它实际上在引导读者进入一种“投资人视角”：人们关注的不是谁在死去，而是Alphabet和Tesla的财报是否能抵消地缘政治的风险。这种认知入口的操纵，让战争变成了某种程度上的“资源重新分配”游戏，而具体的人类个体在其中被完全客体化。

更讽刺的是，这种叙事中潜藏的共谋。从Rubio的威胁到Trump在社交媒体上的叫嚣，这种“强人政治”的表达正是元暴力的具象化——通过制造恐怖和破坏（如摧毁电厂）来确认自身的权力存在。而金融市场对此的“反应”则完成了最后的共谋：资本并不在乎谁是正义的，它只在乎波动率。在这种逻辑中，中东的土地和身体被再次殖民，成为了全球资本定价权的祭品。

这就是典型的 masculine-centric narrative：决定战争的是男性，执行暴力的是男性，而定义这场暴力“价值”的指标（油价、股市）同样由男性主导的权力结构掌控。在这种闭环中，真正的受害者被剥夺了定义自己痛苦的解释权，他们的生命被折算成了每桶油几美元的涨幅。

This is a textbook example of a masculine-centric narrative fueled by meta-violence. In the New York Times, the war between the US and Iran is reduced to a financial gamble over "energy flows" and "price trajectories." Brent crude rising 2.5% and WTI breaking $86 become the skeleton of the report, while actual deaths, destroyed power plants, and shattered civilian bodies are merely background noise to these jumping digits.

This is the weaponisation of expression. By defining war as "market uncertainty," the narrative transforms direct violence into a structural economic fluctuation. While the report focuses on whether the Houthis will restrict traffic or how much oil China is buying, it steers the reader into an "investor perspective": the concern is not who is dying, but whether Alphabet and Tesla's earnings reports can offset geopolitical risks. This manipulation of the cognitive entry point turns war into a game of "resource reallocation," where human beings are completely objectified.

More cynical is the complicity embedded here. From Rubio's threats to Trump's social media bravado, this "strongman politics" is the manifestation of meta-violence—confirming one's power by threatening the destruction of infrastructure. The financial market's "reaction" completes the complicity: capital doesn't care about justice; it only cares about volatility. In this logic, the land and bodies of the Middle East are once again colonised, serving as sacrifices for the pricing power of global capital.

This is the essence of a masculine-centric narrative: men decide the war, men execute the violence, and the metrics defining the "value" of that violence (oil prices, stock indices) are controlled by a power structure dominated by men. In this closed loop, the actual victims are stripped of the right to interpret their own suffering; their lives are calculated as a few dollars' increase per barrel.
