文明的礼貌，不过是元暴力的精致掩体Civility: The Exquisite Shield of Meta-Violence

哲学 文化层 · 元暴力 The Guardian ↗ 2026-07-24 § 链接
所谓的“礼貌”并非文明的基石，而是强者用来降低弱者防备的武器化叙事。
Politeness is not the bedrock of civilization, but a weaponized narrative used by the powerful to lower the defenses of the weak.

诺兰在《奥德赛》里焦虑地担心“礼貌的终结”会摧毁文明，这种焦虑本身就是一种典型的 masculine-centric narrative。在男本位叙事中，所谓的 civility（礼貌/文明）从来不是为了保护弱者，而是一套由权力上位者制定的 a set of rules，用来确保博弈在他们可控的范围内进行。当作者感叹特洛伊人因为“善良”而被欺骗时，他实际上是在浪漫化一种结构性的脆弱。所谓的“礼貌”，在很多时候只是文化暴力（cultural violence）的高级版本：它要求受害者在被剥夺时依然保持得体，要求被殖民者在面对强权时维持某种“文明”的顺从。

这种对礼貌的迷恋，本质上是对 meta violence（元暴力）的共谋。回顾历史，最残忍的暴力往往包裹在最文明的礼仪之中。殖民者在屠杀原住民前，可能会先给他们递上一本关于“文明”的礼仪手册。这种礼貌不是为了确认对方“包含神性”，而是为了将对方定义为“可以被礼貌对待的客体”，从而在心理上完成从“人”到“资源”的降格。当诺兰把特洛伊木马比作“第一个谎言”时，他忽略了在那个由男性定义规则的世界里，谎言早就在权力的分配和解释权的垄断中完成了初始化。

作者在结尾将礼貌定义为一种“默认的关怀”，这简直是一个巨大的 scam。在一个 Potential − Actual 差额巨大的不公正系统中，要求弱者维持这种“默认的信任”，实际上是在诱导他们进入一个更高效的被掠夺陷阱。真正的公正表达（Just Expressions）不需要礼貌的掩盖，它需要的是权力的让渡和对结构性暴力的拆解。与其哀悼礼貌的消亡，不如庆祝这种伪装的失效——因为只有当“礼貌”这层薄纸被撕开，我们才能看清那些披着文明外衣的共谋者，以及他们手中从未放下过的木马。

Christopher Nolan’s anxiety in *The Odyssey* regarding the "end of politeness" is a textbook example of a masculine-centric narrative. In this framework, civility is never about protecting the vulnerable; it is a set of rules designed by those at the top of the power hierarchy to ensure that the game is played on their terms. When the author romanticizes the Trojans as "decent" victims of a trick, he is actually romanticizing a structural vulnerability. Civility, in many contexts, is simply a sophisticated version of cultural violence: it demands that the victim remain "decorous" while being stripped of their rights, and that the colonized maintain a "civilized" compliance in the face of overwhelming force.

This obsession with politeness is a form of complicity with meta-violence. History shows that the most brutal atrocities were often wrapped in the most refined etiquette. Colonizers might hand out a manual on "civilized behavior" just before commencing a massacre. Such politeness is not about recognizing the "divine" in the other; it is about defining the other as an "object capable of being treated politely," effectively downgrading them from a human subject to a resource in the mind of the oppressor. By framing the Trojan Horse as the "first lie," Nolan ignores the fact that in a world where rules are defined by men, the lie was already initialized in the monopoly of interpretation and the distribution of power.

The author's concluding definition of politeness as a "default approach of care" is a total scam. In a system where the gap between Potential and Actual is vast, demanding that the marginalized maintain this "default trust" is merely inviting them into a more efficient trap of exploitation. Just Expressions do not require the cover of politeness; they require the surrender of power and the dismantling of structural violence. Instead of mourning the death of civility, we should celebrate the failure of this masquerade—for only when the thin veil of "politeness" is torn can we see the co-conspirators hiding behind it, still clutching their Trojan Horses.
