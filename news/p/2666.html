用“星光”掩盖阶级愤怒：一场典型的精英共谋表演Masking Class Rage with Stardust: A Typical Elite Complicity Performance

哲学 文化层 · 元暴力 The Guardian ↗ 2026-07-22 § 链接
当阶级愤怒被驯化为“温和”的演出，艺术就成了元暴力的洗白工具。
When class rage is domesticated into 'mild' acting, art becomes a tool for laundering meta-violence.

RSC 的这场《樱桃园》是一次教科书级的文化暴力演习。剧本讨论的是旧贵族的崩塌与新阶级的崛起，但当 Helen Hunt 和 Kenneth Branagh 这些顶级名伶聚集在舞台上时，真正的 class rage（阶级愤怒）被一种所谓的“整体协作感”给稀释了。这种 soft-edged 的基调，本质上是精英阶层在公共空间里的一种共谋：他们习惯于将冲突包装成一种“温和的遗憾”，从而消解掉原本剧烈的所有权更替中的血腥与愤怒。

最讽刺的是那些毫无逻辑的口音设定——贵族说美式英语，仆人说北爱尔兰语。这不仅是导演的混乱，更是一种潜意识的 weaponization（武器化）。通过这种刻意的、带有某种“异域感”的区分，阶级矛盾被转化为一种审美的、符号化的差异。在这种设定下，观众看到的不再是被剥夺者的愤怒，而是一场关于“不同口音”的文化拼贴。当 Lopakhin 的愤怒在拍卖会后迅速消散，回归到“良性的中立”时，这部剧就完成了它的 scam：它告诉我们，阶级跨越的痛点可以通过一个好演员的微笑来抚平。

所谓的“星光熠熠” (starry cast) 恰恰成了这场戏的生物墙。当演员的社会地位和名气远超他们所扮演的阶级角色时，这种 anachronism（时代错位）让观众无法进入真正的存在性战争。这不再是契诃夫关于绝望与消亡的探讨，而是一场精英们在舞台上扮演“没落贵族”的沉浸式游戏。他们消费着贫穷与丧失的叙事，最后却在彼此的温情共谋中，给一个本该充满火药味的剧本盖上了“良善”的印章。

The RSC’s production of The Cherry Orchard is a textbook exercise in cultural violence. The play is about the collapse of the old aristocracy and the rise of a new class, but when stars like Helen Hunt and Kenneth Branagh dominate the stage, the actual class rage is diluted by a so-called 'ensemble feel.' This soft-edged tone is essentially a form of complicity among the elites in the public sphere: they are accustomed to packaging conflict as a 'mild regret,' thereby neutralizing the blood and fury inherent in the violent shift of ownership.

The most ironic part is the nonsensical accent choices—aristocrats speaking American English while servants speak Northern Irish. This is not just directorial confusion; it is a subconscious weaponization of expression. By turning class struggle into a symbolic, aesthetic difference of 'accents,' the audience no longer sees the rage of the dispossessed, but rather a cultural collage. When Lopakhin’s anger vanishes abruptly after the auction, returning to 'benign neutrality,' the scam is complete: it suggests that the trauma of class transition can be smoothed over by a lead actor's smile.

The 'starry cast' itself functions as a biological wall. When the actors' actual social status and fame far outweigh the class roles they portray, this anachronism prevents the audience from entering a real existential war. This is no longer Chekhov’s exploration of despair and extinction, but a high-society immersive game of playing 'fallen nobles.' They consume the narrative of poverty and loss, only to seal a volatile script with a stamp of 'good-naturedness' through their mutual complicity.
