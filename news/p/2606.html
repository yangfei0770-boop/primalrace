好莱坞的“内容”陷阱与资本共谋Hollywood's 'Content' Trap and Capital Complicity

哲学 结构层 · 文化层 The New York Times ↗ 2026-07-21 § 链接
当创意被定义为“内容”，创作者就成了可被AI替换的耗材。
When creativity is defined as 'content', creators become replaceable consumables for AI.

这篇评论揭露了一个典型的结构性暴力过程：好莱坞正在经历从“艺术创作”到“内容生产”的定义权转移。当 Paramount 和 Warner Bros 试图通过合并来追求所谓的 economy of scale 时，他们实际上是在进行一场关于“定价权”的博弈。在资本的叙事里，剧本不再是文学艺术，而被简化为 content——一个可以被量化、被削减、最终被 AI 替代的工业参数。

这是一个典型的共谋场域。顶层的 billionaire 们通过债务驱动的并购，将原本多元的创作生态转化为一个巨大的债务偿还机器。他们口中的“精简公司”和“提高效率”，本质上是 structural violence：通过剥夺中基层创作者的议价能力和生存空间，将 Potential 的艺术可能性压缩为 Actual 的财务报表。作者提到的“fail upward”在 Peak TV 时代是一种幸存者偏差，而现在的 downward mobility 则是系统性收割的必然结果。

最讽刺的是，这种武器化的叙事——“为了行业更好”——正是为了掩盖对主体性的抹杀。当创作者被告知他们是“内容”的一部分时，他们就在潜意识中接受了自己作为耗材的身份。这不仅仅是经济危机，这是一场关于“谁定义什么是创作”的存在性战争。而这场战争的胜者，永远是那些掌握了认知入口和资本杠杆的人。

This essay exposes a classic process of structural violence: Hollywood is undergoing a shift in the power of definition, moving from 'artistic creation' to 'content production.' As Paramount and Warner Bros seek economies of scale through mergers, they are actually gambling for pricing power. In the capitalist narrative, a screenplay is no longer a literary art; it is simplified into 'content'—an industrial parameter that can be quantified, slashed, and eventually replaced by AI.

This is a textbook field of complicity. Top-tier billionaires, driven by debt-fueled acquisitions, are transforming a once-diverse creative ecosystem into a massive debt-repayment machine. Their rhetoric of 'slimming down' and 'increasing efficiency' is, in essence, structural violence: by stripping the bargaining power and survival space of mid-to-low-level creators, they compress the potential of artistic expression into the actuals of a financial balance sheet. The 'fail upward' phenomenon mentioned by the author was merely survivor bias during Peak TV; the current downward mobility is the inevitable result of systemic harvesting.

The most cynical part is the weaponized narrative—claiming this is 'for the good of the industry'—which serves only to mask the erasure of subjectivity. When creators are told they are part of 'content,' they subconsciously accept their identity as consumables. This is not just an economic crisis; it is an existential war over 'who defines creation.' And the winners of this war are always those who control the cognitive gateways and capital leverage.
