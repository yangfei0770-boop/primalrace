释放不等于安全：乌干达的“接吻罪”与结构性恐吓Release is Not Safety: The 'Kissing Crime' and Structural Intimidation in Uganda

国际 直接层 · 结构层 · 文化层 · 元暴力 The Guardian ↗ 2026-07-24 § 链接
Relief is not safety. 法律的表演性撤诉无法抵消结构性暴力的常态化。
Relief is not safety. A performative dismissal of charges cannot erase the normalization of structural violence.

两个年轻女性因为在公共场合接吻而被逮捕，面临终身监禁，最后被释放。很多人会把这看作一个 a good_news，但按照 Violence = Potential − Actual 的公式，这里的 Actual 仅仅是回到了一个被恐惧笼罩的原点，而 Potential 依然被那部反 LGBTQ+ 法案死死压制。差额依然巨大，暴力依然在流动。

这是一次典型的 Structural Violence 与 Cultural Violence 的联动。法律（Anti-Homosexuality Act）提供了直接施暴的合法性，而社区的嘲弄、家人的驱逐则完成了文化层面的共谋。在这种环境下，接吻不再是情感的表达，而是一次极其危险的博弈。当法律允许警察以“影响他人”为由将人投入狭小囚室时，法律本身就成了武器化的叙事入口，旨在通过个案的极端化，制造一种全社会的 chilling effect。

最令人作呕的是这种“释放”的机制。正如活动家所言，她们获释不是因为法律的公正，而是因为外部压力和媒体关注。这意味着，在乌干达，你的生存权不取决于人权，而取决于你是否能获得足够的 visibility 来让统治者在 PR 成本与镇压快感之间做权衡。这不是 justice，这是一种表演性的让步。

她们虽然离开了囚室，但依然生活在一种 meta-violence 之中：她们被定义为“耻辱”，被剥夺了工作能力，被禁锢在一种必须时刻自我审查的恐惧里。只要那部法律还在，只要“男性中心叙事”依然垄断着关于“正常”与“道德”的解释权，这种释放就只是下一次逮捕之前的短暂喘息。

Two young women were arrested and faced life imprisonment for kissing in public, only to be released later. Many would label this as a good_news, but applying the formula Violence = Potential − Actual, the Actual has merely returned to a baseline of pervasive fear, while the Potential remains crushed by the Anti-LGBTQ+ Act. The gap remains vast; the violence continues to flow.

This is a classic linkage between Structural Violence and Cultural Violence. The law provides the legitimacy for direct violence, while community mockery and familial rejection complete the complicity at the cultural level. In this environment, a kiss is no longer an expression of affection, but a high-stakes gamble in an existential war. When the law allows police to imprison people for "influencing others," the law itself becomes a weaponized narrative designed to create a chilling effect across society through extreme individual cases.

The mechanism of this "release" is the most repulsive part. As advocates noted, their freedom was not granted by legal justice, but by external pressure and media attention. This means that in Uganda, your right to exist depends not on human rights, but on whether you can garner enough visibility to force the regime to weigh PR costs against the pleasure of suppression. This is not justice; it is a performative concession.

Though they have left the cell, they remain trapped in meta-violence: branded as "disgraces," stripped of their livelihoods, and confined to a constant state of self-censorship. As long as that law exists and the masculine-centric narrative maintains its monopoly over the definition of "normalcy" and "morality," this release is nothing more than a brief respite before the next arrest.
