用“法律面试”作为逮捕陷阱：国家机器的捕猎艺术The Bait-and-Switch of 'Legal Interviews': The Art of State Hunting

国际 直接层 · 结构层 · 文化层 The Guardian ↗ 2026-07-24 § 链接
将行政救济伪装成逮捕陷阱，是结构暴力向直接暴力的瞬间坍塌。
Disguising arrest traps as administrative relief is the instantaneous collapse of structural violence into direct violence.

这不仅仅是一个关于移民身份或刑事记录的个案，而是一次典型的认知入口武器化。ICE（美国移民海关执法局）通过邀请 Connor Read 参加绿卡面试这一“救济性”叙事，诱导其进入一个绝对受控的物理空间，随即将其转化为逮捕现场。这在本质上是一场不对等的博弈：国家机器利用受害者对“合法化”的渴望作为诱饵，完成了从 structural violence（制度性压迫）到 direct violence（身体禁锢）的无缝切换。

值得关注的是叙事权的争夺。ICE 的发言人在声明中密集使用 “criminal illegal alien” 和 “worst of the worst” 这种高度标签化的词汇。这是一种典型的文化暴力（cultural violence），旨在通过定义 Connor 为“纯粹的罪犯”，来抹除他自九岁起就在美国成长、社会化并建立家庭的生物学与社会学事实。当一个人的身份被简化为一个法律标签，他的主体性就死在了这个标签里，而这种“去人性化”的处理正是为了让随后的强行隔离和遣返显得“正当”。

Connor 所谓的“犯罪记录”——持有毒品设备、持有酒精和抗拒执法——在这样一个高度军事化的执法体系中，被用来作为剥夺其基本生存权的合法性背书。这揭示了权力运作的潜逻辑：法律不是为了公正，而是为了在需要清除特定人口时，提供一套可以被公众接受的、名为“法治”的共谋叙事。在这种叙事中，个体被切割成碎片，而国家机器则在碎片中挑选最有利于其权力扩张的解释权。

This is not merely a case of immigration status or criminal records; it is a textbook weaponisation of the cognitive entry point. By inviting Connor Read to a green card interview—a narrative of 'relief'—ICE lured him into a controlled physical space and instantly converted it into an arrest site. This is an asymmetric game: the state machine uses the victim's longing for 'legitimacy' as bait to execute a seamless transition from structural violence to direct violence.

Notice the struggle for narrative power. The ICE spokesperson densely employs labels like 'criminal illegal alien' and 'worst of the worst.' This is quintessential cultural violence, designed to erase the biological and sociological fact that Connor grew up, socialized, and built a family in the US since age nine. When a human being is reduced to a legal tag, their subjectivity dies within that tag, and this 'dehumanization' is exactly what makes the subsequent detention and deportation appear 'justified.'

Connor's so-called 'criminal history'—possession of narcotic equipment, liquor, and resisting an officer—is used by a militarized enforcement system as a legitimacy endorsement to strip him of his basic right to exist. This reveals the underlying logic of power: the law is not for justice, but to provide a complicity narrative called 'rule of law' that the public can swallow when the state needs to purge specific populations. In this narrative, the individual is fragmented, and the state machine picks the interpretation that best serves its own expansion of power.
